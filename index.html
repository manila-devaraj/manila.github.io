The proliferation of mobile technologies has significantly increased the speed at which users create and manage personal data, yet users often remain unaware of how their data is accessed. While smartphone privacy management relies on permission systems, existing user interfaces frequently fall short of aligning with user privacy expectations. Prior studies have mainly explored runtime permissions, leaving the permissions manager interface, key for privacy control post-installation, under-examined. My dissertation aims to redesign the permissions managers interface to enhance usability and support more efficient and privacy-aware decision-making. It addresses three core challenges: (1) high cognitive load from numerous permissions; (2) absence of prompts for regular review; and (3) limited user understanding of the data accessed by permissions. To improve permission discoverability and comprehension, the proposed redesign will leverage personalization and customization techniques to support more informed user privacy decisions.

Research Projects

Understanding of User Priortitization and Comprehension of Smartphone Permissions

Smartphones allow users to control the sharing of their data with apps according to their privacy preferences. Yet, users struggle to enact their privacy preferences via the available permission settings. We administered an online questionnaire to smartphone users from the United States (ùëõ = 151). We analyzed the ratings and rankings of importance to understand how participants prioritize permissions in terms of absolute and relative importance. We found that prioritization by users is largely based on their usage and privacy concerns. Our findings indicate that users misunderstand permissions and express unfamiliarity with a few permissions.

Parental Oversharing: Investigating ‚ÄúSharenting Concerns‚Äù in the Digital Age

Sharenting refers to the practice of parents sharing information about their children, including photos, videos, and personal details, on social media platforms. We collected data manually from subbreddits a total of 60 posts along with 10 comments per post, which was a total of 600 comments, which was qualitatively analyzed by two authors. Safe Sharenting Practices involved achieveing the need to share their children's journey through private channels such as TinyBeans or Whatsapp, while safeguarding their privacy. Through this reserach, we understand the spectrum of parent's motivations, insights, and behaviors related to sharenting, covering motivations, concerns, perceptions, and strategies related to safe sharenting.

Interdependent Privacy in Smartphone App Permissions

Users may unintentionally expose information about others on their devices when making smartphone permission decisions. In this study, we examine whether users take others‚Äô privacy into account when making permission decisions. Specifically, we investigate whether providing visual cues increases awareness of interdependent privacy, whether concerns differ across permission types, and whether users expect others to consider their privacy in similar situations. This work is a replication study conducted through an online survey and a between-subjects design. Our findings show that participants were generally more concerned about protecting their own privacy than that of others. Importantly, the results indicate that participants were more attentive and responsive to other's privacy when they realized the requested data included information related to others.

Slides Report

Visualizing Open Data: Public Perceptions on Privacy

The reserach focussed on addressing how visualization impacts privacy, specifically exploring how the form of visualization, as opposed to tabular data presentation, affects individual's perception of data privacy. To investigate we conducted survey and semi-structured interviews. We discovered that visualization indeed impacts the perception of data privacy, but this influence often "reinforces existing impressions" rather than completely reversing privacy perceptions. We identified privacy, safety and social concerns expressed when the data was visualized differently other than the tabular format.

Slides Report

Intertemporal Analysis using Symbolic Regression

Humans routinely make choices between alternatives that have consequences unfolding over time. This study aimed to formulate a function that accounts for the discounting model of human decision-making over time. An online questionnaire was developed using Qualtrics that accesses the discounting structural thinking. Three modeling approaches, linear regression, polynomial regression, and a genetic programming algorithm, were applied to derive a symbolic expression of the decision-making function. 3D visualisations was used to explore the relationships among variables and evaluate the accuracy of the models.
